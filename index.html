Welcome to Your Writing Style Analysis

This week we've been exploring why the writing we create as humans matters — and how AI can be a tool that supports our writing instead of replacing it.

In this activity, you're going to find out something surprising: you already have a writing style. You have patterns, habits, and a voice that are uniquely yours, even if you've never thought about them before.

You'll use an AI tool of your choosing to analyze samples of your own writing. Then you'll reflect on what you discover.

Choosing an AI tool: If you don't already have an AI tool you like, go to our AI resources page (opens in new tab) and choose one of the four tools listed. Keep your AI conversation open in another tab.

Before we start, gather some samples of your writing. You want at least 2–3 pieces. These need to be things you actually wrote — not group projects, not templates, not AI-generated text.

  • Your Weekly Page from Week 1
  • Text messages or emails you've written (copy a few longer ones)
  • Social media posts or comments
  • Papers or assignments from other classes
  • Journal entries, blog posts, or any personal writing
  • Work emails or professional writing

A mix of different types works best — it helps the AI see how your voice stays consistent or changes across situations.

Round 1: How You Build Sentences

Go to your AI tool and paste the prompt below. After the prompt, paste all of your writing samples.

Copy this prompt into your AI tool
I'm a college student learning about my own writing style. Below are several samples of my actual writing from different contexts. Please analyze ONLY my sentence structure patterns. Specifically: - How long are my sentences on average? Do I tend toward short, punchy sentences or longer, complex ones? - Do I vary my sentence length, or do they tend to be similar? - How do I typically start my sentences? (With "I"? With dependent clauses? With questions?) - Do I use a lot of connecting words (and, but, so, because)? - Any other structural patterns you notice? Please be specific and use examples from my writing to show me what you mean. Be honest — I want to learn, not just hear nice things. Here is my writing: [PASTE YOUR WRITING SAMPLES HERE]

Now analyze my writing voice and tone. Based on the same writing samples: - How would you describe my overall voice? (Formal? Casual? Somewhere in between?) - Does my voice change between the different samples? How? - Do I sound confident, tentative, conversational, academic, or something else? - Is there a personality that comes through in my writing? What kind of person would a reader imagine wrote this? - What makes my voice different from generic or AI-generated writing? Again, be specific and use examples from my writing.

Round 3: Your Word Choices & Habits

One more round. Same AI conversation:

Copy this prompt into your AI tool
Finally, analyze my word choice and recurring habits. Based on my writing samples: - Are there words or phrases I use a lot? (Even small ones like "just," "really," "I think," "honestly") - Do I use simple, everyday vocabulary or more formal/academic language? - Do I use figurative language (metaphors, comparisons, exaggeration)? - What techniques or moves does my writing make repeatedly? (For example: asking questions, giving examples, using humor, hedging my claims, acknowledging other perspectives) - What are my biggest strengths as a writer based on what you see here? - If you had to describe my writing to someone who'd never read it, what would you say? Be specific. Use examples.

Step 5 of 7

The Moment of Truth

You've now seen your writing analyzed from three different angles. Take a breath.

An AI just told you things about your own writing that you may never have noticed. Some of it probably felt accurate. Some of it might have felt off.

Both reactions matter. Your AI tool can identify patterns in text. But it doesn't know you. It doesn't know why you write the way you do, what experiences shaped your voice, or what you're trying to say underneath the words. That's the difference between analysis and understanding.
Step 6 of 7

Your Turn to Ask

Now it's your turn to drive. Go back to your AI conversation and ask a follow-up question about your writing — anything you're curious about.

Here are some ideas, but ask whatever interests you:

"Why do I start so many sentences with 'I'? Is that a problem?"
"You said my voice is conversational — what would it look like if I tried to write more formally?"
"Can you show me what my writing would sound like if someone else wrote the same ideas?"
"What's one thing I could work on to become a stronger writer?"
